Research Overview

Galactic winds are very important for enriching the metallicity of the circumgalactic medium (CGM) and for regulating the star formation rates of the galaxy. These winds are observed to be multiphase, consisting of gas at a wide range of densities and temperatures. These winds can be moving at speeds of thousands of km/s. The majority of the wind’s volume is made up of hot, ionized gas (about 10^7 K) and the majority of the mass is made up of cold clumps of gas (10^4 K). What’s interesting is that the cold gas is observed to be comoving with the wind.

Modeling the acceleration of the cold gas in galactic winds has raised many challenges. Computer simulations in the past have ended with the cold phase of gas heating up and getting destroyed rather than accelerated and entrained. This is caused by hydrodynamic instabilities like the Kelvin-Helmholtz (KH) and Rayleigh-Taylor instabilities. Cosmological simulations are incapable of resolving these small-scale properties.

More recently, however, researchers have had success in modeling the acceleration of the cool clouds with the implementation of radiative cooling. At the small scale, the KH instability will create mixing in the shear layer between the cloud and wind, and this mixing layer will have an intermediate temperature. Radiative cooling is most efficient at this intermediate temperature, so this mixing layer cools down and accretes onto the cloud. With this model, cool clouds can actually grow and reach the velocity of the background wind, becoming entrained. This model has been very successful, but it has not been explored in high resolutions due to the simulations being very computationally expensive.

These simulations consist of a three dimensional box with a number of cells in the x, y, and z directions. As you increase the resolution, you increase the number of cells of the cloud and of the mixing layer. A better resolved mixing layer could lead to faster mixing and cooling. The effectiveness of the radiative cooling model may be dependent on the resolution of the simulation. My research aims to quantify this relationship by examining the velocities of the clouds at different resolutions.
