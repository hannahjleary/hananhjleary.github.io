The Resolution Dependence of Cloud-Wind Simulations

Background

Galactic winds are very important for enriching the metallicity of the circumgalactic medium (CGM) and for regulating the star formation rates of the galaxy. These winds are observed to be multiphase, consisting of gas at a wide range of densities and temperatures. These winds can be moving at speeds of hundreds of km/s. The majority of the windâ€™s volume is made up of hot, ionized gas (about 10^7 K) and the majority of the mass is made up of cold clumps of gas (10^4 K). What has been of interest to astronomers is how the cool clouds accelerate to become comoving with the wind.

Modeling the interaction between a cool cloud and a hot, fast wind has historically faced challenges. Computer simulations in the past have ended with the cold phase of gas heating up and getting destroyed rather than accelerated and entrained in the wind. This is caused by hydrodynamic instabilities like the Kelvin-Helmholtz (KH) and Rayleigh-Taylor instabilities. Cosmological simulations are incapable of resolving these small-scale processes.

More recently, however, researchers have had success in modeling the acceleration of the clouds with the implementation of radiative cooling. At the small scale, the KH instability will cause mixing to occur in the shear layer between the cloud and wind, and this mixing layer will have an intermediate temperature. Radiative cooling is most efficient at this intermediate temperature, so the mixing layer's mass cools and accretes onto the cloud. With this model, cool clouds can actually grow in mass and reach the velocity of the background wind, becoming entrained.

This model has been very successful, but has not been explored at high resolutions due to the simulations being very computationally expensive. These simulations consist of a three dimensional box with a fixed number of cells in the x, y, and z directions. As you increase the resolution, you increase the number of cells spanning the cloud and mixing layer. A better resolved mixing layer could lead to faster mixing and cooling. Therefore, the effectiveness of the radiative cooling model may be dependent on the resolution of the simulation.

Project Overview

My research aims to quantify this relationship between the resolution of the simulaions and the cloud's acceleration and survival. Determining possible effects of resolution will help decide if this mixing and cooling model is sufficient for modeling cool cloud acceleration in galactic winds, or if other physical mechanisms such as cosmic rays or magnetic fields is necessary. In order to study this relationship, I run cloud-wind simulations on GPU nodes on the CRC supercomputer using Cholla. Cholla is a GPU-based hydrodynamics code that is massively parallel, so it is able to efficiently run very high resolution simulations.
